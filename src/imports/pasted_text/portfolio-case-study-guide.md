Start with the case study card, since this is effectively your portfolio's homepage and the very first thing a recruiter sees before they even click into your work. Treat it like a pitch: it needs one or two real screens from the actual solution (never just a logo or an abstract graphic), a short, specific, action-oriented title rather than something vague like "Mobile App Redesign," and a subtitle that gives a hint of the problem or outcome. Keep the visual style consistent across all your cards so your portfolio reads as one cohesive product rather than a set of mismatched projects — this consistency is what makes someone decide to click in rather than skip past.
Once they click in, the cover or intro page sets the context fast. Use a descriptive title rather than just a brand name — "Zalando App" tells a recruiter nothing, while "Zalando — Ratings & Reviews Redesign for Business Viability" immediately tells them what the project was about and why it mattered. Pair this with a complementary subtitle, clear labels for your role, team, timeline, and platform, and a hero visual that hints at the final product.
Right after that comes the executive summary, which should be scannable in seconds: two to four sentences summarizing the project, a metadata block covering role, team, timeline, and platform, and an early teaser of the outcome or impact so the recruiter has a reason to keep reading. Make it clear here whether this was a real shipped product, a redesign, or a student project, since that context shapes how they'll judge everything that follows.
Next is the problem statement, and this is where you show your thinking rather than just your output. A useful formula is: "[User type] struggle with [specific pain point] because [root cause]. This leads to [negative outcome]. We know this because [evidence — data, research, or support signals]." Cover who was affected and why it mattered, how you actually knew it was a problem, the constraints you were working under (time, tech, team size, budget), and hint at your solution direction early. The biggest mistake designers make here is forcing recruiters to scroll forever before seeing what was built — reveal the direction early instead of saving it as a twist.
After the problem is established, walk through your process, but selectively. Recruiters don't want every sticky note from a 47-post-it brainstorming session; they want the key moments — discover, define, design, test, deliver — narrated through your decisions and reasoning ("I chose X because Y"), including what you explored and ultimately rejected. Showing iterations side by side, v1 versus v2 versus final, does far more to demonstrate growth and judgment than a chronological dump of every step.
Visuals and artefacts should follow a three-tier hierarchy: a hero visual that's the "wow" shot of your final solution, process visuals like sketches and wireframes that show the most insightful moments of your thinking, and evidence visuals like test results and before/after comparisons that back up your claims. Every visual needs a short caption explaining why you made that choice, not just what it looks like, and if you have an interactive prototype, link it here as a bonus that lets recruiters engage rather than just scroll past screenshots.
Close the loop with outcomes and impact — the section most designers rush through, even though it's often what actually closes the deal. State the impact of your work, include metrics if you have them (something like "66.4k HP AI PCs sold" or a "6.3% user adoption rate"), and if you don't have hard numbers, qualitative substitutes work just as well: "5/5 users completed the task without errors," "Product lead approved for development," "Reduced steps from 7 to 3," or "I learned X assumption was wrong because Y." Round it out with what you learned, what you'd do differently next time, and any next steps for the project.
A few cross-cutting habits tie the whole thing together. Keep one cohesive visual style — palette, typography, and grid — applied consistently across your entire portfolio so it feels like a single product rather than disconnected projects. Optimize every page for scanning with short paragraphs, clear headings, and bolded key phrases, since a recruiter should be able to grasp the gist of any section in under sixty seconds. Use descriptive, insight-driven headings instead of generic labels — "Users want AI benefits without compromising privacy" tells a much richer story than simply "Research." Make the case study genuinely engaging by embedding interactive prototypes and clickable links rather than static screenshots alone, and once you've impressed someone, give them an obvious next step by linking to your other case studies so they don't hit a dead end.
The underlying principle across all of this: treat your portfolio like a product and the recruiter like your user. Every component should earn its place, and the story of how you solved a real problem for real people with real evidence will always matter more than the raw artefacts themselves.
